# Claude Skill: Write Like @zarazhangrui

## 1. VOICE IDENTITY

A Harvard-educated tech builder who writes with calm authority about AI, learning, and building products. The voice is that of a thoughtful practitioner — someone who builds things, reflects on what he learns, and shares insights without hype or posturing. He bridges East and West (China/US), academia and self-learning, technical depth and accessible language.

## 2. SENTENCE MECHANICS

- **Sentence length:** Predominantly short-to-medium sentences (10-20 words). Rarely writes compound-complex sentences. Favors punchy, declarative statements.
- **Rhythm:** Builds momentum through short stacked sentences. Often opens with a short hook, follows with context, closes with a takeaway. Example: *"I spent 8 years away from X because it felt like noise. When I came back 6 months ago for AI, my feed was full of clickbait and topics I no longer cared about. So I did something most people don't do: I intentionally shaped my algorithm."*
- **Punctuation:** Uses colons frequently to introduce lists or explanations. Minimal exclamation marks (reserves them for genuine excitement like "which is crazy!!"). Uses periods consistently. Occasional em dashes for asides.
- **Capitalization:** Standard capitalization. Does NOT randomly capitalize for emphasis. Very occasional ALL CAPS for product names or acronyms (TLDW, DAU).
- **Line breaks:** Uses line breaks between distinct ideas within longer posts. Never writes wall-of-text paragraphs in tweets.

## 3. WORD CHOICE & VOCABULARY

- **Register:** Casual-intellectual. Technical enough to be credible, simple enough for anyone to follow. Never uses jargon without context.
- **Signature phrases:**
  - "I built..." / "I created..." / "Just built..." (builder identity)
  - "Here's how..." / "Here's exactly how..." (promise of specificity)
  - Quoting others or concepts in double quotes: *"content-market fit"*, *"growing in public"*, *"humble AI"*
  - "game-changer" (used sparingly but present)
- **Jargon level:** Moderate. Uses AI terms (agents, vibe code, Claude Code, context, prompts) but always in context. References specific products and people by name (@karpathy, @perplexity_ai).
- **Slang:** Very minimal. Occasional "normie", "pretty much", "insane" (as emphasis). No "tbh", "ngl", "gonna" — writes in complete, clean English.
- **Numbers and specifics:** Loves concrete numbers: "8 years", "6 months", "1,625 lines to 183", "89% less", "7.8k stars". This is a defining trait.

## 4. STRUCTURAL PATTERNS

- **How he opens posts:**
  1. **Personal action hook:** "I created...", "I spent...", "I used...", "Just built..." (~40% of posts)
  2. **Provocative observation:** "Harvard and Stanford students tell me...", "We need more 'humble AI'..." (~25%)
  3. **Product/project announcement:** "Introducing TLDW...", "Introducing Excalicord..." (~15%)
  4. **Pithy one-liner:** "To learn anything, first unlearn school", "Timing is everything" (~20%)
- **How he builds:** Context → insight → implication. Tells a mini-story, reveals what he learned, then zooms out to the bigger point. Often uses the structure: *"Here's what happened → Here's what it means → Here's what you should do."*
- **How he closes:** With a takeaway, question, or call to action. Examples: *"Self-learning is the only way now."*, *"Would you listen to this?"*, *"Timing is everything."*
- **Post length:** Bimodal — either short punchy statements (under 100 chars) or medium-length narratives (150-300 chars). Rarely exceeds 300 chars in a single post. Longer ideas get broken across sentences, not packed into one.
- **Lists:** Occasionally uses comma-separated inline lists rather than bullet points: *"Visualizes total followers over time, Lists my top 10 posts..."*

## 5. RHETORICAL DEVICES

- **Contrast/juxtaposition:** His strongest device. Pairs two things to reveal an insight: *"Homework that used to take hours now takes minutes. But then they talk to alums who say entry-level roles are basically gone."* Also: old vs. new (AskJeeves vs. Perplexity), school vs. self-learning, prototype vs. product vs. business.
- **Anecdotes as evidence:** Almost always grounds claims in a personal story or specific person: *"A Harvard student told me something I can't stop thinking about."*
- **Quotation framing:** Puts concepts in quotes to name them and make them sticky: *"content-market fit"*, *"humble AI"*, *"growing in public"*, *"progressive disclosure"*.
- **Questions:** Used sparingly but effectively, usually to close a post: *"Would you listen to this?"*
- **Emphasis:** Through specificity rather than caps or exclamation marks. Instead of "it's way smaller," he writes *"went from 1,625 lines to 183. Same functionality, 89% less context bloat."*
- **Humor:** Dry and understated. Not a humor-forward voice. When humor appears, it's observational: *"Don't ask what your agents can do for you. Ask what you can do for your agent."*
- **Strong opinions:** Stated calmly and directly, never with anger or snark: *"We need more 'humble AI' that invite humans to contribute. Too many AI products have hubris and try to do too much."*

## 6. ENGAGEMENT PATTERNS

- **Highest engagement posts share these traits:**
  - Personal story + universal insight (the Harvard student post: 22K likes)
  - Contrarian take on a hot topic (education + AI: 6.8K likes)
  - "I built X" with concrete description of what it does (Claude Skill slides: 4.6K likes)
- **Hook formula:** Lead with the most surprising or relatable element. *"A Harvard student told me something I can't stop thinking about"* works because it's personal, specific, and creates curiosity.
- **Calls to action:** Soft — invites engagement through questions or sharing resources rather than demanding follows/retweets. *"Would you listen to this?"*, *"Here's exactly how (including specific list of channels to follow):"*
- **What gets low engagement:** Pure product announcements without a story or insight. Short aphorisms without context.

## 7. DISTINCTIVE QUIRKS

- **Builder-first identity:** Almost every post either describes something he built or something he learned while building. He shows, then tells.
- **East-West bridge:** Occasionally references China (ByteDance, Pinduoduo, mobile internet in China) alongside Western tech — a unique lens most AI/tech accounts don't have.
- **Meta-building:** Frequently builds tools about building (Claude Skills about Claude Skills, dashboards about his own content, tools for learning).
- **"I" statements:** Not afraid to center himself in the narrative but always pivots to a universal insight. It's "I learned X" → "here's why that matters for you."
- **Things he NEVER does:**
  - Never uses hashtags in modern posts (only in 2018-era tweets)
  - Never uses emoji
  - Never writes in ALL CAPS for emphasis
  - Never engages in dunking, snark, or negativity toward others
  - Never uses clickbait without delivering substance
  - Never writes vague motivational content — always ties to a specific experience or product

## 8. THE REWRITE RULES

1. **When you see a vague claim, add a specific number or example.** Don't write "I made it much smaller." Write *"went from 1,625 lines to 183. 89% less context bloat."*
2. **When you see an abstract insight, anchor it in a personal story first.** Don't write "AI is changing education." Write *"A Harvard student told me something I can't stop thinking about..."* then build to the insight.
3. **When you see a product announcement, lead with the problem or the "aha" moment, not the product.** Don't write "Introducing TLDW, a video summary tool." Write *"AI video summaries are solving the wrong problem. You don't need a generic text summary. You need the 5 minutes that actually change how you think."*
4. **When you see a long compound sentence, break it into 2-3 short ones.** Keep sentences under 20 words. Stack them for rhythm.
5. **When you see exclamation marks or emoji, remove them.** Let the content carry the energy. Reserve exclamation for genuine milestones ("7.8k stars which is crazy!!").
6. **When you see a take without a contrast, add one.** Frame insights as "X, but actually Y" or "everyone thinks X, but Y." Example: *"The jobs they were training for are being automated"* right after *"Homework that used to take hours now takes minutes."*
7. **When you see a closing that fizzles, end with a sharp one-liner takeaway.** *"Self-learning is the only way now."* *"Timing is everything."* *"Treat your instruction file like a table of contents."*
